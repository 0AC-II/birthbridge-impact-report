01 —

Executive Summary

Finding 01
BirthBridge is reaching women — but not early enough
Only 26.4% of enrolled women joined the program in their first trimester. Most arrivals (62.9%) came in the second trimester, leaving insufficient time to deliver the full ANC protocol and limiting the program's protective potential.
Finding 02
Sokoto is a program within a program — and it needs urgent attention
Sokoto's skilled birth attendance rate stands at 24.7%, compared to 67.9% in Kano. The 43-percentage-point gap cannot be attributed to program design alone — it points to structural barriers that require a targeted, state-specific response strategy.
Finding 03
Referrals are being issued, but not always followed through
64.7% of women were referred for facility delivery — a strong CHW effort — but only 72.2% of those referrals converted to actual attendance. Nearly 1 in 4 referred women delivered without skilled care. Understanding this dropout is the single highest-leverage question in the 2025 program cycle.

BirthBridge's 2024 program reached 4,100 women across 73 communities in Kano, Kaduna, and Sokoto. Community health workers made substantial strides in service delivery — the program's overall ANC4+ completion rate of 57.2% outpaces the national average. Yet the data also surfaces inequities and gaps that, left unaddressed, will constrain impact and donor confidence. This report tells both sides of that story.

The program enrolled women from across all three states, with Kano contributing the highest volume (1,800 pregnancies), followed by Kaduna (1,400) and Sokoto (900). The 18.2% repeat client rate — nearly 1 in 5 enrolled women — is an encouraging signal of community trust and sustained program presence. These are women who experienced BirthBridge once and returned for a subsequent pregnancy.

The WHO recommends initiating antenatal care before 12 weeks. BirthBridge's first-trimester enrollment rate of 26.4% reflects the difficulty of early detection in low-resource community settings — but also signals an opportunity. Programs that systematically improve community mobilization prior to conception or in early pregnancy typically see first-trimester rates climb to 40–50%.
03 —

What We Delivered

57.2% ANC4+ completion rate
3.28 Avg ANC visits per woman
67.9% Danger sign counseling
64.7% Delivery referral rate

The program's core service delivery indicators tell a story of real effort and meaningful output. A 57.2% ANC4+ completion rate — meaning more than half of enrolled women attended the minimum four antenatal contacts recommended by WHO — represents genuine progress in a context where the national average hovers around 45–50%. Danger sign counseling, an often-overlooked educational intervention, reached 67.9% of enrolled women, equipping them with knowledge that can save lives in the absence of immediate clinical care.

CHW visit adherence — the proportion of scheduled visits that were actually completed — averaged 63%, with Kano (75.3%) substantially outperforming Sokoto (42.6%). This operational gap is not incidental; it maps directly onto the outcome disparities explored in the next section.

⚠ Data Quality Signal
Overall data completeness stands at 80%, but Sokoto forms recorded only 60.8% completeness — meaning roughly 4 in 10 data fields submitted from Sokoto were blank or inconsistent. This figure almost certainly means that Sokoto's outcomes are underrepresented in this analysis. Poor data quality is not just an M&E problem: it is a program management problem that limits the organization's ability to detect and respond to crises in its lowest-performing geography.

"Of every 100 women who enrolled in BirthBridge, only 23 reached the end of the full newborn follow-up protocol. That is a program that saves lives — and a system that leaves too many behind."

The most severe attrition point in the care continuum is the transition from delivery to neonatal follow-up. While skilled birth attendance and PNC rates hold at roughly 50%, neonatal follow-up completion — requiring CHW visits on Day 1, Day 3, and Day 7 — drops to just 22.6%. The causes are likely multiple: CHW workload, distance, poor postnatal scheduling systems, and mothers' mobility after delivery. This is an area warranting dedicated program design attention in the next cycle.

The referral gap: 64.7% of women were referred for delivery. Of those referred, 72.2% actually attended a facility. This means approximately 1,200 women received a referral in 2024 that they did not act on — and delivered without skilled care. Understanding why (cost, distance, family decision-making, distrust of facilities) should be a research priority before 2025 programming is finalized.

Where We're Strongest — and Where We're Not

Aggregate figures can mask the most important story in the data. When results are disaggregated by state, BirthBridge reveals itself to be not one program, but three — with dramatically different performance profiles across Kano, Kaduna, and Sokoto.

The Kano-Sokoto gap is the defining story of BirthBridge's 2024 data. At 24.7% skilled birth attendance, Sokoto is performing at roughly one-third the level of Kano — despite operating under the same program model, the same CHW training curriculum, and the same M&E framework. This divergence signals that contextual and structural factors — facility availability, road infrastructure, cultural norms around male involvement in birth decisions, CHW supervision quality — are driving outcomes more than program design. Sokoto needs a contextually tailored response, not simply more of the same.

Kaduna sits in a troubling middle position: its indicators are moderate, but the trend direction matters. Without specific investment, Kaduna may drift closer to Sokoto than Kano. Its visit adherence rate of 60.3% and data completeness of 76.7% suggest a program that is functioning, but under strain.

06 —

What the Data Tells Us to Do Next

The following recommendations are rooted directly in the 2024 program data. They are not generic best practices — they respond to specific gaps and performance patterns observed in BirthBridge's own results. Priority level reflects both magnitude of impact and feasibility within the existing program structure.

▲ High Priority
Launch a Sokoto-Specific Program Recovery Plan
Treat Sokoto as a distinct program context requiring tailored strategy. Commission a rapid qualitative assessment to identify barriers to facility delivery and CHW visit follow-through. Consider deploying a dedicated state mentor, revising CHW incentive structures, and introducing community-level demand generation through women's groups. Data systems must be rebuilt here before outcomes can be reliably measured.
Based on: 24.7% SBA rate · 42.6% visit adherence · 60.8% data completeness
▲ High Priority
Investigate and Address the Referral-Attendance Gap
~1,200 referrals issued in 2024 did not result in facility attendance. Before issuing more referrals in 2025, BirthBridge must understand why. A structured exit survey or community dialogue with referred women who did not attend will surface whether barriers are financial (transport, facility fees), social (husband/family opposition), or logistical (facility quality, distance). The findings should directly reshape how CHWs discuss and coordinate referrals.
Based on: 64.7% referral rate · 72.2% referral-to-attendance conversion
● Medium Priority
Redesign the Postnatal Care Protocol to Protect Newborns
With only 22.6% of newborns receiving all three follow-up visits (Day 1, Day 3, Day 7), the program's postnatal care chain is fragile. Solutions likely include: scheduling postnatal visits at the time of enrollment (not delivery), assigning a specific CHW responsible for each newborn's first week, and introducing a simple SMS or paper-based alert system to flag missed visits within 24 hours of the expected contact.
Based on: 52.3% PNC-48hr rate · 22.6% neonatal follow-up completion
● Medium Priority
Build a Community Mobilization Strategy for Earlier Enrollment
Only 26.4% of women enrolled before their second trimester. Late enrollment is both a program quality issue and a missed opportunity — earlier enrollment directly enables higher ANC completion rates. Strategies to test include: community pregnancy detection networks, training traditional birth attendants and community leaders as referral agents, and partnering with religious leaders who are trusted messengers in first-trimester health decisions in these geographies.
Based on: 26.4% first-trimester enrollment · avg gestational age at enrollment: 18 weeks
